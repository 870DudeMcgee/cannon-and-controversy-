# 02 Production: Episode 09

- episode title: How the Canon Was Shaped
- episode code: ep-09_how-the-canon-was-shaped
- runtime target: 10 to 16 minutes for the first documentary cut
- governing argument: The canon was not settled by one book list, one rabbi, one council, or one reformer. It emerged through a long sequence of stabilizations, arguments, translations, council lists, confessional decrees, and print-distribution choices.
- tone priority: culmination and chronology first, controversy second

## Core Thesis

This episode should argue that the Bible list Christians inherited is the result of a multi-century process. Jewish consolidation, early Christian practice, regional councils, translators, reformers, and modern printing societies each changed the practical shape of the canon.

It should function as the direct closing episode to the full documentary block: after Episode 08 identified the removed books themselves, Episode 09 explains when, where, and by whom the canon was narrowed, widened, stabilized, and finally normalized in common print culture.

## Suggested Shot Order

1. Open on the claim that the canon was shaped across centuries.
2. Mark Palestinian canon closure as an early stabilization point.
3. Present the Septuagint as the Greek Bible of diaspora Judaism and early Christianity.
4. Present Yavneh or Jamnia as rabbinic consolidation after 70 AD rather than a single neat council.
5. Chapter break into early Christian uncertainty.
6. Present Muratorian Fragment, Irenaeus, Origen, and Athanasius as evidence of real debate and mixed practice.
7. Chapter break into formal Christian canon decisions.
8. Present Rome, Hippo, Carthage, and Innocent I as the strongest early Christian list tradition.
9. Present Jerome as the translator who planted durable seeds of doubt inside the Latin tradition.
10. Present Florence as the wider pre-Reformation reaffirmation.
11. Chapter break into Reformation and post-Reformation closure.
12. Present Leipzig and Luther's 1534 Apocrypha section.
13. Present Trent as Catholic closure and Westminster as formal Protestant exclusion.
14. Present the British and Foreign Bible Society as the point where the books largely vanish from ordinary Protestant print culture.
15. Close on the line that the canon was shaped by a chain of decisions, not one moment.
