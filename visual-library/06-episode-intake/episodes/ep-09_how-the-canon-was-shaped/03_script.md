## Delivery Rule

Read this as the closing chronology of the series. The voice should feel final, lucid, and restrained. Let the dates accumulate clarity rather than urgency.

## Act 1: Early Formation and the Septuagint World

### Beat 1

By the end of this series, one point should be clear. The canon did not drop out of history all at once. It was shaped across centuries.

### Beat 2

The story begins with early stabilization in the Hebrew tradition, then with the Septuagint in Alexandria, where Jewish scriptures entered the Greek world and became the Bible of the diaspora and, later, of early Christians.

### Beat 3

After the destruction of Jerusalem in 70 AD, rabbinic Judaism consolidated around a narrower canon. Modern scholars debate whether Jamnia was a single council, but the larger point stands: the Christian and rabbinic scriptural worlds were no longer moving in exactly the same direction.

## Act 2: Christian Witness and Real Uncertainty

### Beat 4

Early Christianity did not inherit a fully uncontested list either. The Muratorian Fragment shows debate still alive in the second century, and Irenaeus stands as an early witness to the wider Greek scriptural world.

### Beat 5

Origen is the perfect sign of the tension. He can list a narrower canon, yet still use Tobit, Judith, and the Greek Daniel material as scripture in practice. Athanasius then draws another distinction, preserving the now-familiar New Testament list while treating the disputed Old Testament books as a second tier.

## Act 3: Rome, Hippo, Carthage, and Jerome

### Beat 6

In the late fourth and early fifth centuries, formal Christian lists become much clearer. Rome, Hippo, Carthage, and Innocent I all stand on the side of the larger canon.

### Beat 7

Then comes Jerome. He translated under Church authority, but he also privileged the Hebrew canon and wrote prefaces that would keep suspicion alive for centuries. He is not the whole story, but he becomes a hinge in it.

### Beat 8

Florence later reaffirms the wider list before the Reformation, showing that the larger canon was not a late emergency invention. It was the established inheritance of Western Christianity long before Luther.

## Act 4: Reformation, Confession, and Print Disappearance

### Beat 9

At Leipzig in 1519, the fight becomes explicit. Luther is pressed with 2 Maccabees on prayers for the dead, and his answer is not mainly to reinterpret the verse but to demote the book.

### Beat 10

In his 1534 Bible he moves the books into an Apocrypha section, still calling them profitable and good to read. Trent responds by defining them as sacred and canonical. Westminster goes further than Luther and denies them authority in the Church altogether.

### Beat 11

But even then, the books do not disappear overnight. The final practical break comes in the nineteenth century when the British and Foreign Bible Society stops printing them in low-cost Protestant Bibles. That is when absence becomes normal for ordinary readers.

### Beat 12

So the canon story is not one moment, one villain, or one decree. It is a chain of decisions across centuries, and those decisions shaped the Bible millions inherited without ever seeing the whole process behind it.
