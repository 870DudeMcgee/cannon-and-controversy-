## Act 1: Two Stages After Death

### Beat 1

Episode 5 argued that disputed books remain inside biblical language. Episode 6 turns that claim into a doctrine question.

### Beat 2

One of the first questions many readers ask is simple. If people are already with God or in judgment after death, why does scripture still speak about the dead rising later from their graves?

### Beat 3

Historic Christian readings usually answer that question by distinguishing two stages, not one: an intermediate state after death and before the end, and then a final resurrection and public judgment.

### Beat 4

That is why Catholics, Orthodox, and most Protestants can all affirm some kind of post-death consciousness while still insisting that resurrection matters. The body is raised. Judgment is made public. Creation is renewed.

### Beat 5

So the issue in this episode is not whether Christians believe in resurrection. The issue is what happens to the doctrine map when one side removes the clearest purification texts but keeps the broader afterlife tension.

## Act 2: Luke 16 and the Intermediate State

### Beat 6

The key New Testament text is Luke 16, the rich man and Lazarus.

### Beat 7

This passage matters because it gives the most vivid picture of post-death condition before final resurrection: comfort for Lazarus, torment for the rich man, conscious memory, moral awareness, and a gulf that cannot be crossed.

### Beat 8

That means death does not erase identity. Justice continues. Moral history is not suspended until the last day.

### Beat 9

At the same time, Luke 16 does not by itself prove later technical purgatory. Catholics and Protestants both know that. The passage shows an intermediate state, but not the whole map.

### Beat 10

Still, it does something important. It rules out the simplest one-stage model and forces readers to reckon with a layered afterlife: present condition now and final resurrection later.

## Act 3: 1 Enoch 22 and the Structured Realm of the Dead

### Beat 11

This is where 1 Enoch 22 becomes unusually useful.

### Beat 12

In that vision, the realm of the dead is not one flat undifferentiated Sheol. It is structured. The righteous and the wicked are separated. Conditions differ. Memory persists. Final judgment is still ahead.

### Beat 13

Read against that background, Luke 16 feels much less isolated. Its great gulf fixed, its moral separation, and its waiting-for-final-judgment logic all fit the same Second Temple conceptual world.

### Beat 14

That matters historically because it shows why New Testament afterlife language can sound both immediate and future-oriented at the same time. The dead are already in a morally differentiated state, but final resurrection still completes the story.

## Act 4: Canon Choices Shape Doctrine Outcomes

### Beat 15

Now the consequence sharpens. The clearest text for prayers for the dead and post-death purification is 2 Maccabees 12:44 through 46.

### Beat 16

The clearest short statement about the souls of the righteous after death is Wisdom 3:1 through 4. Both texts speak with unusual directness. Both are absent from Protestant Bibles.

### Beat 17

But the broader tension remains. Revelation 20 still speaks of the dead being raised. John 5 still speaks of those in the graves hearing the Son's voice. First Corinthians 15 still builds a full theology of bodily resurrection. Luke 16 still suggests conscious post-death existence before that final day.

### Beat 18

So a methodological shift takes place. Remove the explicit purification text. Keep the broader afterlife tension. Then build a narrower model from what remains.

### Beat 19

That does not automatically mean dishonesty. It means canon choices shape doctrine outcomes. Different traditions are not reasoning from the same textual base.

### Beat 20

And that is the core claim of Episode 6. The canon debate does not only decide what books are printed in the Bible. It changes what Christians can say most directly about death, judgment, purification, and hope.
